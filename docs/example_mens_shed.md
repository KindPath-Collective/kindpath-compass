# KindPath Compass — Worked Example: Men's Shed Scenario

*This example demonstrates the Compass in action within a community social work context.*

---

## The Situation

*(As a student practitioner might present it in supervision)*

"I've been working with a Men's Shed in the Northern Rivers. There's one bloke, mid-60s, been coming for about eight months. He's reliable — shows up every Tuesday and Thursday without fail, works well with the tools, gets along fine with everyone. But last month his wife died, and since then he's been coming more often — sometimes every day — and staying later. He doesn't talk about it. If anyone brings it up, he nods once and says 'yeah, it's tough' and goes back to what he was doing. The other men don't really know what to do, so they mostly leave it. I'm not sure what to do either. Do I need to refer him somewhere? Is he OK? Am I missing something?"

---

## Compass Analysis

### MOVE 1 — TEXTURE RECOGNITION

*What is actually present here?*

What's present is a man who has lost his primary relational anchor and has responded by increasing his attendance at the only place that functions as a relational field for him — a place where interaction is structured around shared task, not emotional disclosure, and where nothing is required of him except showing up and working.

He is not presenting with grief. He is presenting with **purposeful containment**. The daily attendance is not dissociation or avoidance — it is, on its own terms, a sophisticated adaptive response. The Shed is giving him structure, safe male presence, and the particular gift of a task that needs his hands and not his feelings.

The silence when the subject is raised is not shutdown. It is regulation. "Yeah, it's tough" and returning to work is saying: *I know you see it. I'm not ready to hold it with language yet. Let me hold it with wood and metal.*

The practitioner's discomfort ("I'm not sure what to do, do I need to refer him somewhere?") is tracking something real — but what it's tracking is not a problem with the participant. It's the gap between a grief that is present and the absence of a conventional place to put it.

---

### MOVE 2 — EMOTIONAL ARCHITECTURE

*What is structurally holding this in place?*

**The Shed is load-bearing.** For many men of this generation, the Men's Shed is the single primary community structure — the place where masculine identity, useful contribution, and quiet relational belonging coexist. It is not a secondary resource. For him, right now, it is the structure.

**The language of feeling is not the only language of processing.** The architecture here includes a generational and cultural context in which emotional disclosure is not the primary mode of metabolising loss. He has a working model: you feel things while doing things. You don't separate them and then analyse them. This is not incomplete coping — it is a different architecture.

**The other men are also in the structure.** They don't know what to do, so they leave him alone — which is, functionally, what he needs them to do. That awkward "not knowing" has produced the right containing behaviour. It is worth naming this to the group quietly, not as a lesson but as acknowledgment: *you're doing something right.*

**Referral is a question of fit, not protocol.** The instinct to refer comes from a framework where grief = counselling referral. That is not wrong, but it's not the only right. A grief counsellor who requires verbal disclosure of emotional content in one-to-one sessions may, for this person, be the wrong fit. The Shed may be the right therapeutic environment for this man's particular architecture.

---

### MOVE 3 — ZPB REIMAGINING

*What would genuine care as governing logic produce here?*

Genuine care would:

**Hold the Shed as a therapeutic environment, not just a community program.** Name this explicitly in how it is resourced and supported. If the coordinator needs support in navigating grief in a community setting, that is a legitimate professional development need — not a gap in the program.

**Not impose disclosure.** The worst possible outcome here is a well-meaning intervention that makes him feel surveilled, pathologised, or obligated to perform emotional availability he is not yet ready for. ZPB means trusting his architecture unless there is evidence of real harm.

**Create low-pressure contact.** Not "how are you really doing" — but "what are you working on this week?" The relational door is held open through task and presence, not through invitation to disclose.

**Watch for deterioration signals, not for grief itself.** Grief is not the indicator for concern. The indicators for concern are: withdrawal from the Shed suddenly, signs of self-harm risk, not eating, not sleeping, stopping engagement with things that previously mattered. If he shifts from increased engagement to withdrawal, that is the signal.

**Acknowledge to yourself (in supervision)** that you are sitting with uncertainty and that's exactly where you should be. The discomfort you feel is appropriate — it is the honest signal that something real is happening and you don't control how it moves. That is not a failure of practice. That is practice.

---

## Notes for Practitioners

This scenario is common in Men's Shed settings and in any community program that serves older men. A few specific points:

**Men's grief often looks like busyness.** Increased task engagement, more time at community sites, practical problem-solving about the deceased's affairs — these are not avoidance, they are the texture of grief for many men.

**You are not responsible for his processing.** You are responsible for holding a space in which he can be present. Those are different jobs.

**The referral question is worth holding open, not closed.** After two or three months, if the intensity hasn't shifted, a gentle conversation about what he finds helpful — framed around *him*, not around *your concern* — may open options. "Some blokes find it useful to talk to someone who's had a lot of experience with this. Others don't. Are you getting what you need at the moment?" is a usable frame.

**This is what the Compass is for.** The three moves produce not a plan, but a clearer view. The practitioner who worked through this is no longer asking "do I need to refer him?" because they've answered the prior question: *what is actually present here?* The referral question can be held with much less anxiety once the texture is clear.
